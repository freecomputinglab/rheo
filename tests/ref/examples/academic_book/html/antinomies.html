Antinomies of Perpetual Peace

The Republic of Morality

The wars of revolutionary France provoked in Germany the most famous philosophical response to them, namely the Kantian theory of peace1. Although an outline of this theory can be found in Kant’s first treatise in the philosophy of history, entitled Idea powszechnej historii w aspekcie kosmopolitycznym (Idee zur einer allgemeinen Geschichte in weltbürgerlicher Absicht)2 from 1784, it is expressed in full form in works that directly react to the war of 1792‒1795. Above all, two texts are at issue: O porzekadle: To może być słuszne w teorii, ale nic nie jest warte w praktyce (Über den Gemeinspruch: Das mag in der Theorie richtig sein, taugt aber nicht für die Praxis) (1793) and, of course, Do wiecznego pokoju (Zum ewigen Frieden) (1795). Proper understanding of the project of “perpetual peace”3 requires placing it within the broader context of Kant’s political thought4, focused around two problems: history and the relation of politics and morality.

History is part of nature—this is the fundamental thesis of Kant’s philosophy of history. This means that the subject of history is the human species as a natural kind which constantly moves—according to nature’s own laws—toward progress, that is, toward achieving the full development of the human being’s possibilities and features, the crowning of creation. History, therefore, is a process of perfecting the human as a species, the gradual realization of its original predispositions. In other words, “[in – B.W.] the distant future the human species will finally work out a state in which all the seeds that nature has placed in it can fully develop, and its vocation here on earth will be fulfilled”5. Yet the most important potential that nature has deposited in the human is its spontaneous freedom, which transcends “the mechanical order of animal existence”6. Nature thus creates a human being whose most proper possibility of being itself is freedom understood as causa sui, being one’s own cause—what is most natural in the human turns out to be its capacity to negate natural determination. Nature’s true goal, its highest progress, manifests itself in human freedom, which is at once the negation and full realization of nature. This paradox can be cast in the form of the Kantian antinomia historii: we must think of progress in history noumenally, as the activity of a free human, and at the same time we must think of it phenomenally, as the necessary work of nature itself7.

The intelligible freedom of the human and the necessary order of nature remain inseparably conjoined. A clear-cut answer is therefore undecidable as to whether history is guided by the manifest deeds of humans or the hidden ruse of nature8. Most forcefully this logic and its built-in ambiguity are expressed in the famous concept of “unsocial sociability”—the unavoidable antagonism of individuals as the means selected by nature itself for the realization of humankind’s general species-ends (that is, ultimately, liberation from that very nature, gained in the world of culture as a “second nature”). As Kant argues:

The means that nature uses so that all predispositions [of humanity – B.W.] may develop is antagonism in society insofar as it ultimately becomes the source of a law-giving order. By antagonism I understand here the unsocial sociability of human beings (die ungesellige Geselligkeitt der Menschen), i.e., their inclination to live in society, which, however, is combined with an all‑too‑general resistance that constantly threatens to break up society. (…) At this point the first true step occurs from barbarism to culture, which in essence consists in the cultivation of the social values of the human9.

Kant proposes to resolve the metaphysical antinomy of freedom and nature, which in the social plane finds its expression in the split between politics and morality, through the idea of the Enlightenment state of reason as a kingdom of ends (Reich der Zwecke)10. The problem of the very grounding of politics in morality and the mutual interaction of the two spheres is one of the key issues for contemporary scholars of the Königsberg philosopher’s social philosophy11. It is generally claimed that the theory of peace provides evidence for the unity between “pure” practical reason—morality—and its “application” in history and society—politics. There is a strict relation between the a priori legislation of practical reason and the realization of those laws in the sphere of politics, in the form of the ideas of just law and justice12. Morality without politics is powerless, while politics without morality is blind. Pure practical reason poses two questions: the moral one—“what is lawful; what ought we to do?” and the political one—“how to secure the law’s binding force; how to realize what ought to be?”. In the “Appendix” to Do wiecznego pokoju we read:

In true politics, therefore, not a single step can be taken before paying homage to morality, and although politics in itself is a difficult art, reconciling it with morality is no art at all, for morality cuts the knot that politics cannot untie, insofar as the two remain in mutual contradiction. Human right must be recognized as sacred, even if this recognition costs the ruling power very great sacrifices13.

We see how the three elementary concepts of Kant’s practical philosophy—morality, law, and politics—mediate one another. Ethics, whose paramount category is the universal validity of just law, in this context constitutes the rational form of politics. Reducing politics to morality does not at all mean turning away from action in the real world.

It is, however, clear—notes Étienne Balibar—that politics in Kant’s notion lies neither on the side of morality and duty, nor on the side of law and its effectiveness, but precisely in the linkage, the practical “synthesis” of these two independent elements. It consists in governing people in such a way that they act according to these two criteria14.

The political community of free citizens turns out to be the medium of mediation between politics and morality. Kant posits two demands for such a community: the system of every civil state should be republican (but not democratic-egalitarian), and relations between states should be regulated within a cosmopolitan federation guaranteeing perpetual peace. The internal civic freedom and the external cosmopolitan peace overlap as transcendental conditions of a perfectly just order in which “reason alone ought to rule”15. According to Habermas, the basis of such an order—the enlightened and at the same time enlightening state—is “publicity,” constitutive of the “public sphere” (Öffentlichkeit)16. In the state that is the synthesis of the civil constitution and perpetual peace, “all political actions must, in the public dimension, be derivable from that which constitutes the basis of norms, which, in turn, have been demonstrated before public opinion to be universal and rational norms”17. Politics is then transformed into morality. Politics becomes morality (“lawful in general”), and morality gains political efficacy (“the rule of the norm”) only within the framework of the cosmopolitan republic, which realizes universal law and perpetual peace18. In other words, this project can be described as a cosmopolitan public sphere in which citizens make “public use of their own reason”19.

The Kantian Enlightenment republic is a liberal state under the rule of law that comes into being “in bourgeois homes”20. It is born out of the world of literary salons and discussion clubs where educated citizens discourse on the politics and culture of their surrounding world. This circulation of thought is conditioned by the economic infrastructure of the public sphere, which rests on the free competition of consumers and owners. The autonomy of the Kantian citizen, the participant in the public, “puts down roots in the sphere of commodity exchange”21. The circulation of ideas among users of reason finds its “earthly basis” in free market competition. Therefore, for Kant, being a citizen means fulfilling one condition (excluding the fact that children and women were excluded from this concept):

namely, that he be master of himself (sui iuris), and, in connection with this, that he possess some property (one can also include here any art, craft, the fine arts, or the sciences) from which he is able to support himself (…); and thus that he serve no one other than the community in the proper sense of the word service22.

Accordingly, the wage worker—whose only commodity for exchange is labor power and not owned goods—and the plebeian, who has nothing to exchange, are excluded from the civic public sphere. The kingdom of ends thus turns out to be a petty-bourgeois23 merchants’ republic, and the condition of the resonating citoyen turns out to be the working bourgeois. The modern prosaic Bürger dresses up in the garb of the virtuous ancient polites. In short, the citizen of the public is an abstraction of the burgher; he produces opinions instead of commodities. Kant thus becomes the leading ideologue24 and the philosophical “founding father” of the liberal model of the bourgeois public sphere, which is founded on the suppression of plebeian variants of publicity25. The consequence for the philosophy of the state is as follows: the empirical people (embracing citizens and non-citizens, burghers and plebeians) constitute at once a premise for the emergence of the state and an obstacle to its formation26. Within the state of reason—the synthesis of politics and morality—this people must be transformed from above, through Enlightenment reforms, so that each becomes a citizen guided by the categorical imperative. Thanks to this, the empirical people are turned into the noumenal people—civil society (bürgerliche Gesellschaft)27. In this process the universal state plays the role of the supreme Enlightenment educator; it is the collective incarnation of the Kantian intellectual28, who teaches people their own rights, presents them with the rules of their collective freedom, that is, the freedom of the autonomous individual limited only by the freedom of others. The state thus ultimately provides the framework for the social reproduction of the liberal system of the mutually competing citoyen-bourgeois in the market of commodities and opinions. Only within this ideological horizon can we consider the project of perpetual peace, born of the spirit of commerce29.

The Peace of Enlightenment

The problematic of war and peace, taken in the moral-political sense, appears for the first time in the already mentioned treatise Idea powszechnej historii w aspekcie kosmopolitycznym, written by Kant in Königsberg in 1784, at the end of the reign of the “soldier-king” Frederick II the Great, who transformed his state into a barracked military power. Frederick’s Prussia built its international position thanks to victorious wars, while remaining civilizationally, economically, and socially a backward country, mired in feudal relations. It is truly difficult to imagine a more adequate place on the map of Europe at that time for philosophically reflecting on the essence and character of war, especially as the philosopher still remembered well the devastation brought by one of the bloodiest and most senseless wars of modern Europe, the Seven Years’ War, which, incidentally, elevated Prussia to the rank of a world power.

In thesis seven of his text, Kant observes that the establishment of a perfect civil constitution (the republic of reason) requires the regulation of “external relations between states”30. Nature, acting as the force that drives history, uses antagonism between people and entire societies so that, through a state of wars and conflicts, it ultimately leads to peace. The road to a league of nations, a cosmopolitan federation, runs through barbarism playing out on the stage of history. He notes:

Thus all wars (to be sure, not in the intentions of human beings, but in the intentions of nature) are attempts to introduce new relations between states by destroying, or at least partitioning, old political organisms and creating new ones, which in turn, whether for internal or external reasons, will also be unable to sustain themselves and therefore must undergo revolutions31.

Only the establishment of a federal civil constitution can break this destructive spiral of progress. Here Kant points to the teleological structure of history, which makes use of wars up to now in order to bring about the end of all wars—that is, the point at which war becomes impossible. Why is such a state necessary from the point of view of his conception of history? Because waging wars does not allow the realization of the human species’ natural predispositions, since it hampers the free existence of particular nations, which are always exposed to the risk of destruction in the turmoil of war.

In other words: although, because all the forces of one community are consumed by armaments against another, because of the devastation caused by wars, and, above all, because of the necessity of remaining constantly prepared for them, the full development of predispositions is hampered. At the same time, the resulting sufferings compel our species to find, for the mutual opposition of states against one another, which in itself is salutary and which arises from their freedom, laws of balance and a united authority. This, in turn, will become its certification (Nachdruck), and thus it will introduce the cosmopolitan state of universal security for all states (…)32.

The critique of war proceeds here along two tracks. From the transcendental perspective, war is a disturbance of the teleological development of the human species, since it prevents the constitution of the republican system in each state, which provides the politico-legal frame for the rational and moral life of the individual. The condition of possibility of Kant’s moral civil order is therefore perpetual peace. From the phenomenal perspective, war entails destructive consequences in the everyday existence of nations, causing devastation and mass suffering and, as such, embodies anti-civilizational barbarism. It is also economically disadvantageous for the state, as it consumes resources that should be invested in the educational project of public Enlightenment (as we read in thesis eight of the treatise):

And thus, although, for example, the rulers of our world have no money for universal educational institutions and, in general, for everything that concerns the good of the world, because all resources have already been earmarked in advance for future wars (…). Finally, war itself is slowly becoming not only an undertaking complicated and uncertain in its outcome for both sides, but also very risky because of its effect, namely, the growing war debts (this new invention) whose repayment is not envisaged33.

And, as he adds in the treatise O porzekadle: To może być słuszne w teorii, ale nic nie jest warte w praktyce (from 1793, i.e., already after the outbreak of the revolutionary war), the increasing number of wars leads to greater costs34—related to larger armies and expensive military technologies: “No peace lasts long enough for the savings made during it to equal the expenditures on the next war, and the invention of the national debt is indeed an ingenious, but in the final reckoning self‑destructive, means”35.

Abstracting from Kant’s naive historiosophical optimism—heralding, less than ten years before the outbreak of Europe’s “twenty‑year war,” the proximity of a “universal cosmopolitan state”—his critique of the technical-practical aspect of war (high costs, incurred debts, uncertain investment of capital, and so on) anticipates the typical liberal critique of armament and military policy. For the modern bourgeois, the risk of destroying the sacred foundation of property that wars bring threatens only his interests. The capitalist commercial society, in the mouths of its early ideologues, will in theory be distrustful of wars36, although social practice will show rather that wars can be quite profitable, especially when the idea of a common global market remains a projection of the future. It is interesting that, on the occasion of the economic dimension of war, Kant points to the mechanism of the national debt which, for liberal supporters of laissez faire—to this day—represents the most oppressive face of the state behemoth, thus “robbing” its citizens.

In 1795, when France signed the Peace of Basel with Prussia, Kant published his most important treatise in political philosophy, namely Do wiecznego pokoju37. This coincidence is certainly no accident, for the text formally imitates contemporary peace treaties; it is divided into: “preliminary articles” (Präliminarartikel), “definitive articles” (Definitivartikel), and “secret articles” (Geheimartikel). The first of these, six in number, define the preliminary conditions for establishing universal peace—some of them are stricte formal-legal in character (for example, the first, distinguishing a truce from peace38), while others are politico-moral (for example, the third—the postulate of abolishing the standing army, which constitutes a constant potential for the outbreak of war39—or the sixth, prohibiting immoral methods of waging war40). The preliminary articles project solutions that should be introduced within existing relations between states as a basis for the full realization of the pacifist idea. Achieving perpetual peace requires changing the very form of conducting wars and concluding agreements. Hence Kant writes: “A certain degree of trust in the enemy’s way of thinking must be preserved even in the midst of hostilities, for otherwise no peace could be concluded, and hostilities would turn into a war of extermination”41. Without mutual trust between states at war—understood as the capacity to keep an agreement—the very prospect of peace cannot be conceived.

The three Definitive Articles delineate the necessary conditions for perpetual peace (in the three domains of the validity of law—domestic, international, and cosmopolitan). If war is the wild state of nature for the human being, then peace is a moral task to be established42 for the civil constitution:

  1. In the relation of the citizen to the state this will be the republican order, which for Kant is the transcendental basis of every empirical state43. Republicanism signifies a “mode of governance” (in accord with universal legislation, and thus guaranteeing peace44), not a “form of government” (here, in line with the ancient tradition, he distinguishes: monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy). As we have already noted, the republic of reason is by no means a democracy, which, in the Aristotelian spirit, is identified with the despotism of the majority. Consequently, the form most conducive to the civic republic is the constitutional monarchy, within which the king is the representative of the nation as a whole (and not of its majority, as in democracy, or of its minority, as in the rule of aristocrats)45.

  2. In relations between states it will be a “league of nations”—a cosmopolitan federation which is not at all a world state but its opposite: “instead of the positive idea of a world republic, only a negative surrogate of an ever‑expanding league, lasting and protecting against war, is able to hold back the stream of hostile and law‑evading inclinations, and even that with the constant danger that alliances may collapse”46. Among interpreters, controversy is raised by the question of the powers that the “federal center” would have over other states. Could it coerce them into observing international law? How could it exert such coercion? Finally, would not such a center, focused on a single most enlightened nation (“happily constituted as a republic”47), turn into the despotic world state from which it was supposed to protect? Kant leaves these problems without an unambiguous resolution48.

  3. In the relation of the citizen to the cosmopolitan order. Key to maintaining universal peace is the observance of the right to hospitality (Hospitalität), that is, the universal right to migration. There is no area of the earth that would be the exclusive property of a given community, nation, or individual—therefore the newcomer may not be treated with hostility49. The proper result of the utopia of perpetual peace would be such a global order in which exchange—commercial, cultural, and social alike—would take place without any obstacles, and the intensity of interpersonal ties would increase thanks to the development of locomotion, communication, and information flows. Kant thus anticipates the liberal idea of globalization—a world community in which “the violation of law in one place is felt everywhere”50.

Besides the proper articles, the treatise contains two addenda—“On the Guarantee of Perpetual Peace” and a “secret article”—of which the first is particularly noteworthy, for it contains the philosophical justification of the entire project. There Kant develops his key idea of the philosophy of history (already presented in the Idei powszechniej historii…) of nature as a naturalized providence51, realizing in history its ultimate end—the “moralization” of the human. War is inscribed into this naturalist‑teleological narrative, the Kantian historiosophy, becoming responsible for driving people into the farthest corners of the earth and for establishing the original legal relations between them52. However, in the modern era, in which the bourgeois spirit of commerce reigns and the feudal warrior has been replaced by the bourgeois upstart, war ought to be displaced by peace.

Just as nature, on the one hand, wisely separated nations, among which the will of each state—even one grounded in international law itself—would gladly like, by cunning or force, to subordinate other states, so, on the other hand, it unites, by the power of mutual, advantageous interest, those nations which the concept of cosmopolitan right would not protect from violence and war. This is the spirit of commerce, which cannot coexist with war and will sooner or later take hold of every nation. Since, of all the powers (means) subordinate to state authority, the power of money proves the most reliable, states feel compelled (naturally not by moral motives) to support an honest peace and, wherever war would threaten in the world, to prevent it through their mediation, as if they were in a lasting alliance for this purpose; for great alliances formed for war can, by their nature, very rarely be realized, and even more rarely be successful. In this way, through the very mechanism of human inclinations, nature guarantees perpetual peace (…)53.

In the foregoing argument, Kant maintained that it is nature which, through the universal civil republic established by humans, carries the promise of perpetual peace. Yet in the passage above one can discern the hidden material condition of Kant’s project of peace: “the power of money proves the most reliable.” Thus it is the rule of capitalist social relations, the global market, that turns out to be the foundation of the rule of law and morality in the cosmopolitan aspect. The very idea of cosmopolitanism is therefore a cryptonym of world capital—the more trade, the more international circulation of goods and money, the closer to universal peace. Kant again appears as a leading ideologue of the bourgeois-capitalist public sphere. It is not the finite human and the always limited deeds of the individual, nor even nature, the ruse steering history, but precisely money that possesses the real power to establish peace: “What I am not able to do as a human, what all the forces of my individual being are not able to do, I am able to do with the help of money”54.
